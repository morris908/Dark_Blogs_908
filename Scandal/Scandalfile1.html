Integrity on Trial: Ernst & Young's $100 Million Lesson in Ethics

Ernst & Young (EY), one of the largest auditing firms in the world, has agreed to pay a hefty fine of $100 million after U.S. securities regulators discovered that many of its auditors had cheated on ethics exams. These exams are essential for auditors to obtain or maintain their professional licenses. The firm was found to have not done enough to prevent this cheating.

What Happened?

  • Cheating on Ethics Exams: Hundreds of EY auditors were found to have cheated on ethics exams, which are crucial for ensuring that they uphold professional standards. This cheating occurred between 2017 and 2021.
  • Largest Fine Ever: The $100 million penalty is the largest ever imposed by the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) on an auditing firm. This is significant because auditing firms play a critical role in verifying the accuracy of financial statements for companies and protecting investors from misleading information.
  • Misleading Investigators: The SEC reported that EY misled investigators and withheld evidence during the investigation. This behavior is particularly concerning because auditors are expected to maintain high ethical standards.
  • Internal Complaints Ignored: EY received internal complaints about cheating but did not disclose this information to the SEC initially. As the investigation progressed, it became clear that the cheating was more widespread than initially thought.
  • Specific Incidents: The SEC found that 49 auditors received the answers to an ethics exam required for becoming certified public accountants. Additionally, many other employees cheated on continuing education exams necessary for maintaining their licenses.
  • Reasons for Cheating: Some employees admitted to cheating due to heavy work commitments or repeated failures to pass the exams.

EY's Response

EY acknowledged that its actions were wrong and emphasized the importance of integrity and ethics in its operations. The firm stated that sharing answers on exams violates its Code of Conduct and that it would enhance its efforts to enforce ethical compliance.

Background Context

  • Historical Issues: This is not the first time EY has faced scrutiny for cheating. A similar scandal occurred from 2012 to 2015, which the firm managed internally without external intervention.
  • Regulatory Oversight: The SEC has been closely monitoring accounting firms since the collapse of Enron in 2001, which highlighted the need for strict ethical standards in auditing. Following that scandal, Congress established the Public Company Accounting Oversight Board (PCAOB) to oversee audit firms and enforce compliance with ethical rules.

Future Actions

  • As part of the settlement, EY will hire two independent consultants. One will review the firm's ethics policies, and the other will investigate why the firm failed to disclose the cheating adequately.
  • The SEC's investigation is ongoing, which may lead to further actions against individuals involved in the cheating.

Conclusion

The SEC's settlement with EY sends a strong message that integrity failures by independent auditors will not be tolerated. The case highlights the importance of ethical behavior in the auditing profession, as these firms are responsible for ensuring the accuracy of financial information that investors rely on.
